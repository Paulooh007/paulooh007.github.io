Paul Okewunmi

Hi, I’m Paul Okewunmi. I’m currently exploring independent and community-driven research at ML Collective and Cohere Labs. Previously, I was the founding AI engineer at 5.Y, an AI startup building autonomous agents for customer engagement. There, I developed the first iteration of the agents as well as an evaluation framework for testing them before deployment.

Before that, I worked on one of Meta AI’s open-source projects, LASER, under Kevin Hefferman and David Dale through the MLH Fellowship. I was a core contributor to laser-encoders, the first Python library for generating multilingual embeddings with LASER.

I graduated with First Class Honours in Electrical and Electronic Engineering from Obafemi Awolowo University in 2024. During my undergraduate studies, I contributed to research on UAV precision landing using computer vision and control engineering techniques at A2IR2 Labs under Dr. K. P. Ayodele. I also served as a Teaching Assistant for EEE503: Control Systems Engineering, helping students understand advanced topics such as state-space analysis, stability criteria, and frequency-domain methods.

My research interests lie at the intersection of language models and low-resource languages, particularly African languages. These settings represent a textbook case of ML efficiency: data is scarce, and access to massive state-of-the-art models is often impractical. I focus on adapting efficiency-oriented techniques; smaller, faster, and more data-efficient models, to make breakthroughs in low-resource contexts. In addition, I am interested in autonomous agents and the critical question of how to evaluate them effectively in real-world deployments. This interest was shaped by my role at 5.Y, where I saw firsthand the challenges of building and testing agents designed for real-world use cases.

Publications

  1. ACL 2025
    Evaluating Robustness of LLMs to Typographical Noise in Yorùbá QA
    P Okewunmi, F James, O Fajemila
    AfricaNLP Workshop (Best Paper Award).
    Abs PDF

    Generative AI models are primarily accessed through chat interfaces, where user queries often contain typographical errors. While these models perform well in English, their robustness to noisy inputs in low-resource languages like Yorùbá remains underexplored. This work investigates a Yorùbá question-answering (QA) task by introducing synthetic typographical noise into clean inputs. We design a probabilistic noise injection strategy that simulates realistic human typos. In our experiments, each character in a clean sentence is independently altered, with noise levels ranging from 10% to 40%. We evaluate performance across three strong multilingual models using two complementary metrics: (1) a multilingual BERTScore to assess semantic similarity between outputs on clean and noisy inputs, and (2) an LLM-as-judge approach, where the best Yorùbá-capable model rates fluency, comprehension, and accuracy on a 1–5 scale. Results show that while English QA performance degrades gradually, Yorùbá QA suffers a sharper decline. At 40% noise, GPT-4o experiences over a 50% drop in comprehension ability, with similar declines for Gemini 2.0 Flash and Claude 3.7 Sonnet. We conclude with recommendations for noise-aware training and dedicated noisy Yorùbá benchmarks to enhance LLM robustness in low-resource settings.

  2. IEEE 2024
    Accelerometry-Based Improvement of F450 Quadcopter Flight Control
    S Akinola, P Okewunmi, M Eghrudje
    5th International Conference on Electro-Computing Technologies for Humanity (NIGERCON)
    Abs PDF

    This paper presents a comprehensive analysis of an enhanced quadcopter flight control system integrating accelerometer feedback in the F450 platform. We introduce a novel sensor fusion algorithm combining traditional gyroscopic data with accelerometer feedback [5], implementing an extended Kalman filter for optimal state estimation. Our experimental results demonstrate significant improvements: 37% reduction in attitude deviation under wind disturbances, 42% improvement in hover stability, and 15% reduction in power consumption. The study includes the overall overview of the F450 frame fabrication, detailed mathematical modeling, hardware implementation guidelines, and performance analysis under various environmental conditions. Additionally, we explore advanced control techniques including adaptive gain scheduling and machine learning-based parameter optimization.

  3. NJTD 2025
    Adaptive‑Size Fiducial‑Marker Detection with Dual‑Stage Kalman Filtering for Precision UAV Landing
    K.P. Ayodele, P. Okewunmi, S. Akinola, Abiodun A. Ogunseye, M. Eghrudje et al.
    Nigerian Journal of Technological Development
    Abs PDF

    Precision UAV landing systems face significant challenges with traditional fixed-marker approaches, particularly under varying environmental conditions and integration scenarios. This paper presents a novel approach combining dynamically-sized fiducial markers with dual-stage Kalman filtering to address these limitations. The methodology utilizes a 13.3-inch e-ink display showing ArUco markers that dynamically scale from 16.7 cm to 2.8 cm during descent, actively reducing marker size while accounting for e-ink display refresh constraints through predictive state estimation. A vision system processes frames at 30Hz through an optimized detection pipeline, while the dual-stage Kalman filter handles both vehicle state estimation and parameter adaptation, managing display transition dynamics. The system's universal protocol framework enables compatibility with both integrated and unknown UAV platforms through a dual-mode detection approach, combining passive visual detection with active altitude signalling. Experimental validation using a DJI F450 platform with downward-facing camera demonstrates the system achieves mean landing errors of 1.95 cm under wind conditions up to 15 km/h, representing a 59% improvement over fixed-marker approaches. The e-ink display enables reliable marker detection up to 14.2m altitude under direct sunlight conditions, outperforming LCD displays which become undetectable in bright conditions. Pixel-wise RMSE improved from 150.3 to 140.5 (x-axis) and 170.8 to 151.4 (y-axis) over four sequential landings, demonstrating robust operation across varying integration scenarios.
